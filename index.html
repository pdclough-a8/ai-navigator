AI Navigator - Overview
Quick orientation and usage tips

The AI Navigator provides a way of characterising and viewing AI systems from different perspectives to help you better understand AI. The interactive page maps systems across two families of dimensions. Use the bullets below to get started or jump straight into the page.

-system is

  • Layers - the system's technical anatomy, from how it works to what it does (capabilities, deployment context, paradigm, learning approach, training data, algorithm).
  • Scope - how general the underlying intelligence is (narrow, general-purpose, or hypothetical AGI).
  • Autonomy - how independently the system acts, from human-driven to fully autonomous.

How to evaluate it, and when

  • Lenses - how to evaluate the system (ethics, security, business value, etc.).
  • Lifecycle - when each concern is most actionable (define, data, build, evaluate, deploy, operate).

Tip: click a lens to see its lifecycle relevance, and click the i icon on any card for further details.

Definition (AI System)

AI is a machine-based system that infers a mapping from data and/or knowledge through learning or reasoning to produce outputs - such as predictions, decisions, or content - in order to perform tasks that typically require human cognition, with varying autonomy and, sometimes, adaptiveness.

Distinguishing features of AI
The main distinguising feature of an AI system is that it infers a mapping from data and/or knowledge to produce outputs, rather than computing an exact result over a fully specified structure. AI systems are also machine-based, seek to achieve specific objectives/goals, can operate with some degree of autonomy, and can adapt to new situations. AI systems also commonly have the ability to learn from data However, determining what counts as AI can be complex as the notion of AI is subjective, the AI Effect changes how we perceive AI capabilities over time and people often claim AI capabilities that may not fully meet the definition (known as AI Washing). See further: EU guidance on what counts as AI

Because of the challenges of defining AI, it is often better to view what counts as AI on a spectrum rather than a binary in/out.

i Task names don’t determine AI status – “forecasting”, “optimisation”, and “AI-powered insights” can all be done with or without inference. The algorithm is also not a key determinant of AI-ness - although Machine Learning (especially Deep Learning) is often used to power AI systems but symbolic, logic, optimisation and genetic approaches can also be used.

Notice that two systems can share a scope (both Narrow) yet differ completely across every other layer – and a General-purpose system isn't automatically more autonomous or higher-stakes than a Narrow one. Notice too that the Recommender and the Self-driving car share the same autonomy tag despite one being low-stakes and the other safety-critical – autonomy tells you how independently a system acts, not how much is at stake when it does. Characterising by dimension, rather than a single label, is the point.
